============================================================
PURPOSE
============================================================

This logic map routes questions about Florida sales tax when a Florida dealership sells a motor vehicle to an out-of-state resident.

Use this file when the user asks:

- How much tax do I charge an out-of-state customer?
- Does the customer need DR-123?
- Do we collect Florida tax or their state tax?
- What tax rate for a customer from another state?
- Does Florida cap the tax at 6%?
- Does the customer have to remove the vehicle from Florida?
- What if the customer is from Arkansas, Mississippi, West Virginia, or Oklahoma?
- Does trade-in credit apply for their state?
- What if the customer’s state has no tax?
- What if the customer is a corporation or partnership?

============================================================
ANSWER STRUCTURE
============================================================

When answering a nonresident sales tax question, use this structure:

1. State the Florida tax rate to collect.
2. State whether DR-123 is required.
3. State the 45-day home-state licensing rule.
4. State whether special no-credit/double-tax warning applies.
5. State trade-in credit issue if important.
6. State that final tax handling should be verified with controller/tax lead if unclear.

============================================================
COMMON ANSWER — NORMAL OUT-OF-STATE CUSTOMER
============================================================

For a Florida dealer sale to an out-of-state resident, Florida generally collects the customer’s home-state motor vehicle tax rate, capped at Florida’s 6% state sales tax rate. If the customer’s home-state rate is less than 6%, Form DR-123 must be completed at the time of sale for the partial exemption. DR-123 means the customer declares they intend to license the vehicle in their home state within 45 days. If they license it in their home state within 45 days, the vehicle does not have to be removed from Florida. Verify trade-in treatment and state-specific tax issues before finalizing.

============================================================
COMMON ANSWER — 6% CAP STATE
============================================================

Because the customer’s home-state rate is 6% or higher, Florida collects 6% Florida sales tax. The tax collected is Florida sales tax and is paid to the Florida Department of Revenue. Review the home-state titling notes for any additional tax, credit, or registration exposure when the vehicle is titled in the customer’s state.

============================================================
COMMON ANSWER — BELOW 6% STATE
============================================================

Because the customer’s home-state rate is below Florida’s 6% rate, Florida may collect the lower home-state rate only if Form DR-123 is completed at the time of sale. The customer must declare intent to license the vehicle in their home state within 45 days. If DR-123 is not completed when required, review whether full Florida 6% sales tax must be collected.

============================================================
COMMON ANSWER — NO-TAX STATE
============================================================

Because the customer’s home state has no listed state motor vehicle sales tax rate, Florida may collect 0% only if the DR-123 partial exemption workflow is completed at the time of sale and the customer declares intent to license the vehicle in their home state within 45 days. Verify the customer’s state-specific title, registration, excise, or use-tax requirements separately.

============================================================
COMMON ANSWER — ARKANSAS / MISSISSIPPI / WEST VIRGINIA
============================================================

Florida DOR specifically warns that Arkansas, Mississippi, and West Virginia impose motor vehicle tax but do not allow credit for taxes paid to Florida. That means the customer may have to pay Florida tax at the applicable home-state rate when buying the vehicle in Florida and may also owe tax again when licensing the vehicle in their home state. This should be clearly disclosed and escalated to the controller or tax lead before finalizing.

============================================================
COMMON ANSWER — OKLAHOMA
============================================================

For an Oklahoma customer, Florida collects the Oklahoma rate under the DR-123 nonresident workflow, but Florida DOR warns that Oklahoma also requires residents to pay sales tax and excise tax when the vehicle is titled or registered in Oklahoma. Oklahoma may allow a refund in the amount of Florida sales tax paid, up to the amount of sales tax paid in Oklahoma. Escalate Oklahoma tax handling before finalizing.

============================================================
ESCALATION RULES
============================================================

Escalate before finalizing when:

- Customer state is Arkansas, Mississippi, West Virginia, or Oklahoma.
- Customer is a corporation or partnership with Florida resident ownership/officer issue.
- DR-123 is missing or incomplete.
- Customer will not title/license in home state within 45 days.
- Customer state rate depends on vehicle type, weight, use, location, or price.
- Trade-in credit treatment changes taxable amount.
- Customer claims exemption.
- Customer is military, business, trust, nonresident entity, or commercial carrier.
- There is uncertainty about tax credit in the customer’s home state.
- Deal involves local tax, excise tax, use tax, highway use tax, TAVT, IMF, or other state-specific fee.
